## Session Overview

**Topic:** A mobile or web skincare advisory application that acts like a senior skincare consultant or dermatologist-style assistant.

**Goals:** Generate and refine product ideas around personalized skincare routines, skin type and skin issue assessment, budget-aware product recommendations, local product availability, ingredient explanations, and scientific evidence behind recommendations.

### Session Setup

The session will explore a skincare advice product that can assess customer skin type, skin concerns, budget, and local product availability, then suggest routines and products with ingredient-level explanations and scientific rationale.

## Technique Selection

**Approach:** AI-Recommended Techniques

**Analysis Context:** A mobile or web skincare advisory application with focus on personalization, credible product recommendations, ingredient explanations, local product availability, budget fit, and scientific evidence.

**Recommended Techniques:**

- **Question Storming:** Recommended first because the product involves trust, safety, evidence, and personalization. The goal is to identify the most important questions before proposing solutions.
- **Role Playing:** Recommended second to explore needs from the perspective of customers, skincare professionals, product brands, retailers, and compliance stakeholders.
- **Morphological Analysis:** Recommended third to map product variables such as skin type, concern, climate, budget, ingredient sensitivity, evidence level, geography, and routine complexity into useful product combinations.

**AI Rationale:** The session needs both creative breadth and product rigor. This sequence starts by defining the problem space, expands it through stakeholder viewpoints, and then turns the resulting ideas into a structured product concept map.

## Technique Execution Results

**Question Storming:**

- **Interactive Focus:** Trust questions for an MVP skincare advisor based on a short customer questionnaire.
- **Key Breakthroughs:** The MVP should classify likely skin type and common cosmetic concerns, but avoid claiming reliable allergy or medical-condition diagnosis. The questionnaire design becomes a core product asset because it must be short, easy, and accurate enough to personalize routines.

**Captured Ideas:**

**[Trust #1]**: Short Diagnostic Questionnaire
_Concept_: The MVP begins with a compact questionnaire that asks users to select skin type and skincare concerns. It should classify common cosmetic needs without pretending to diagnose medical conditions.
_Novelty_: The questionnaire is not just onboarding; it is the product's trust foundation.

**[Trust #2]**: Clear Escalation Boundary
_Concept_: The app should stop normal product recommendation flow when users report burning, redness, swelling, serious irritation, or other concerning symptoms, and route them toward professional care guidance.
_Novelty_: Trust is built partly by knowing when not to recommend products.

**[Trust #3]**: Scientific Ingredient Explanation
_Concept_: Recommendations should explain ingredient functions in plain language with evidence-based reasoning, so users understand why each product is suggested.
_Novelty_: This competes against influencers and store assistants by showing transparent reasoning rather than pure persuasion.

**[Trust #4]**: Independent Recommendation Positioning
_Concept_: The app can position itself as serving the customer's skin needs rather than sales KPIs for certain brands. It should make the recommendation logic visible so users see that products are chosen for fit, not brand pressure.
_Novelty_: The app competes with store assistants by making commercial independence part of the trust promise.

**[Trust #5]**: Wider Product Coverage
_Concept_: Unlike a store assistant limited to the products physically available in one shop, the app can compare local products across stores, online channels, and price tiers.
_Novelty_: The app becomes a market-aware skincare advisor rather than a single-store sales channel.

**[Trust #6]**: Product Memory and Ingredient Recall
_Concept_: The app can remember product ingredients, user skin profile, past reactions, and routine history, then use that memory for better recommendations over time.
_Novelty_: This turns ingredient knowledge into a durable user advantage that a human assistant cannot reliably replicate at scale.

**[Trust #7]**: Reaction and Outcome Tracking Loop
_Concept_: The app asks users to report improvements, side effects, irritation, dryness, acne changes, or other outcomes after using a recommended routine. Those results update future recommendations so the app learns what actually works for each client.
_Novelty_: The product becomes an adaptive skincare advisor, not a one-time product recommender.

**[Trust #8]**: Progressive Photo Tracking
_Concept_: Photo upload can help users compare skin changes over time, but the MVP should introduce it carefully as optional progress journaling rather than automated diagnosis.
_Novelty_: The app can create visual accountability and improvement evidence while avoiding premature medical-image analysis claims.

**Role Playing:**

- **Interactive Focus:** Testing the product through stakeholder perspectives, beginning with a budget-conscious acne customer with oily-combination skin and possible barrier damage.
- **Key Breakthroughs:** The product must show that it can repair the skin barrier while improving acne and oiliness, explain conflicts in the user's current routine, and create a progress loop so users keep using it after receiving the first useful routine.

**Captured Ideas:**

**[Role #1]**: Barrier-First Acne Plan
_Concept_: For acne users with possible over-treatment, the app should recommend a barrier-first routine before aggressive acne actives. It can explain that calming irritation and repairing the barrier may be necessary before stronger acne treatment works well.
_Novelty_: This differs from common acne advice that jumps directly to stronger actives and may worsen irritation.

**[Role #2]**: Current Routine Conflict Detector
_Concept_: The app should let users enter their current products, then detect conflicts such as too many exfoliants, harsh cleanser plus retinoid, or acne actives layered too aggressively.
_Novelty_: The app improves trust by explaining what is wrong with the existing routine before selling a new one.

**[Role #3]**: Retention Through Progress Loop
_Concept_: Users may leave once they find a routine, so the app needs check-ins, progress tracking, routine adjustment, seasonal updates, and product replacement suggestions.
_Novelty_: The app's long-term value comes from adaptation and monitoring, not only the first recommendation.

**[Role #4]**: Sensitive Skin Slow-Start Mode
_Concept_: For sensitive users, the app should prioritize dermatologist-tested, low-irritation, fragrance-free, barrier-supporting products and avoid recommending strong anti-aging actives too early.
_Novelty_: The app earns trust by deliberately slowing down recommendations instead of maximizing product intensity.

**[Role #5]**: Calming Explanation Layer
_Concept_: Recommendations for sensitive skin should explain why a product is likely to be gentle, what irritants are avoided, and how to introduce new products gradually.
_Novelty_: The explanation itself becomes part of the product experience because it reduces fear and improves adherence.

**[Role #6]**: Budget Core Routine
_Concept_: For budget-conscious users, the app should prioritize the minimum effective routine: cleanse, hydrate, and protect, then add one targeted active only when budget and tolerance allow.
_Novelty_: The app saves money by recommending fewer, better-fit products rather than a full expensive routine.

**[Role #7]**: Price-Tier Routine Versions
_Concept_: The app should provide low, medium, and premium versions of the same routine, explaining tradeoffs in ingredients, texture, evidence, formulation quality, and local availability.
_Novelty_: Users can choose based on budget without feeling that cheaper options are automatically inferior.

**[Role #8]**: Waste-Avoidance Value Proposition
_Concept_: Budget-conscious users may pay for the app if it prevents wrong purchases, saves research time, and reduces product waste.
_Novelty_: The paid value is framed as cost avoidance, not luxury advice.

**[Role #9]**: Dermatologist-Respect Boundary
_Concept_: The app should earn professional respect by avoiding brand bias, explaining ingredients accurately, not positioning itself above dermatologists, and routing users to professional care when symptoms may be serious.
_Novelty_: The app builds credibility by limiting its own scope, not by claiming to solve every skin issue.

**[Role #10]**: Claim-Safety Guardrails
_Concept_: The product should avoid medical diagnosis and treatment claims in the MVP, especially claims that it can diagnose conditions from photos, treat disease, cure acne, or replace clinical judgment.
_Novelty_: Compliance-aware wording becomes a product design constraint that protects trust and reduces regulatory risk.

**[Role #11]**: Trust-Preserving Brand Exposure
_Concept_: Brands and retailers can benefit when genuinely suitable products are recommended, but the app should refuse paid manipulation of routine recommendations.
_Novelty_: Commercial partnerships are allowed only around data quality, catalog presence, sampling, education, or clearly labeled promotions, not recommendation bias.

**[Role #12]**: Mixed Monetization With Recommendation Firewall
_Concept_: The business model can mix user subscriptions, affiliate revenue, retailer partnerships, and brand partnerships, but recommendation ranking must remain based on skin fit, ingredients, budget, availability, and user outcomes.
_Novelty_: A recommendation firewall becomes a strategic trust feature and business-model guardrail.

**[Role #13]**: Cosmetic Scientist Recommendation Voice
_Concept_: The MVP should recommend products based on client needs with ingredient and skin-type explanations written in a professional cosmetic scientist style.
_Novelty_: The tone differentiates the app from influencers and sales assistants by sounding evidence-based, precise, and neutral.

**[Role #14]**: Day-One Product Data Minimum
_Concept_: The first catalog should include product ingredients, brand, local availability, bio/natural claims, dermatologist-tested claims, cruelty-free status, and recommendation-relevant attributes.
_Novelty_: Trust depends on structured product facts, not only product names and marketing descriptions.

**[Role #15]**: Manual Expert-In-The-Loop MVP
_Concept_: In the earliest version, some recommendation review, ingredient tagging, product matching, or edge-case handling can be done manually behind the scenes before full automation exists.
_Novelty_: The app can validate demand and recommendation quality before building a complete automated recommendation engine.

**[Role #16]**: Life-Change Recommender
_Concept_: Returning users come back when seasons, environment, skin condition, life situation, budget, age, or product curiosity changes. The app should translate those changes into routine adjustments.
_Novelty_: The app becomes a living skincare system rather than a static routine generator.

**[Role #17]**: Persistent Skin Memory
_Concept_: The app should remember questionnaire answers, age, preferences, gender, skin concerns, sensitivities, budget, product reactions, routine history, product likes/dislikes, and outcome tracking.
_Novelty_: The longer the user stays, the more personalized and valuable the app becomes.

**[Role #18]**: Ingredient Intelligence Monitor
_Concept_: A premium feature could monitor new ingredients, new product launches, reformulations, evidence updates, and better alternatives for the user's skin profile.
_Novelty_: The app gives users ongoing expert surveillance of the skincare market, not just one-time advice.

**[Role #19]**: Privacy-First Skin Profile
_Concept_: The MVP should require only the data needed for recommendations while keeping sensitive fields optional, including photos, exact age, gender, precise location, medical history, pregnancy status, and full product history.
_Novelty_: Optionality reduces onboarding friction and makes privacy part of the product experience.

**[Role #20]**: Photo Privacy Controls
_Concept_: Users should be able to track progress without photos, upload cropped skin-area photos instead of full-face photos, and delete photos anytime.
_Novelty_: The app can support visual progress tracking without forcing users into high-sensitivity data sharing.

**[Role #21]**: Skin Data Trust Promise
_Concept_: The app should clearly explain what data is collected, why it is collected, what is optional, and how users can delete it. It should promise not to sell personal skin data.
_Novelty_: Privacy becomes a competitive advantage in a category where users may be hesitant to share face and skin data.

**[Role #22]**: Ingredient Knowledge Sources
_Concept_: For the first market, the app can use authoritative ingredient references such as the European Commission's CosIng database and COSMILE Europe for ingredient names, functions, and consumer-friendly ingredient explanations.
_Novelty_: The ingredient explanation layer can be grounded in credible external references rather than brand marketing claims.

**[Role #23]**: Local Product Availability Pipeline
_Concept_: Product availability can be gathered from major local skincare retailers and pharmacies such as parapharmacy platforms, Sephora, Nocibe, and Marionnaud, but this should respect platform terms and may require official feeds, affiliate APIs, partnerships, or manual catalog curation.
_Novelty_: The app's local-market advantage depends on combining ingredient intelligence with retailer availability, not only generic product knowledge.

**[Role #24]**: Formula Refresh Monitoring
_Concept_: A 6-month product data refresh is a reasonable starting cadence for stable catalog information, but high-traffic or high-risk products should have faster review if users report formula changes, stock changes, or ingredient mismatches.
_Novelty_: Formula freshness becomes part of trust and quality assurance.

**[Role #25]**: Product Function Tagging
_Concept_: MVP product matching should tag product functions and keywords such as calming, exfoliating, hydrating, regenerating, barrier repair, brightening, oil control, acne support, anti-aging, sunscreen, and sensitive-skin suitable.
_Novelty_: Product tags translate long ingredient lists into recommendation logic the user can understand.

**[Role #26]**: Trusted Consultant First Screen
_Concept_: The first screen should position the app as a reliable, trusted skincare consultant that creates personalized routines with ingredient-based explanations.
_Novelty_: The opening promise sells trust and personalization, not only AI novelty.

**[Role #27]**: Short Onboarding Questionnaire
_Concept_: The MVP onboarding should aim for 7-8 core questions, with optional advanced details after the first recommendation.
_Novelty_: The questionnaire can feel quick while still collecting enough data for a credible first routine.

**[Role #28]**: Sample Recommendation Preview
_Concept_: Before asking users to commit, the app should show an example recommendation card or demo routine so users understand the quality of the output.
_Novelty_: Showing the reasoning style early builds trust before data collection.

**[Role #29]**: Signup Timing Test
_Concept_: Signup can happen before the first routine, but a lower-friction alternative is to generate a preview first and require signup to save the routine, track outcomes, or access product links.
_Novelty_: Signup timing becomes a conversion experiment rather than a fixed assumption.

**[Role #30]**: Neutral Ingredient-Aware Differentiation
_Concept_: Customers choose the app over TikTok, Google, Reddit, store assistants, or brand ecosystems because it knows products, ingredients, client profiles, and remains neutral.
_Novelty_: The strategic position is not entertainment or retail power; it is personalized, ingredient-based, unbiased routine reasoning.

**[Role #31]**: Competitor Advantage Awareness
_Concept_: Competitors may have stronger social influence, face-to-face experience, community discussion, or brand-backed retail power.
_Novelty_: The app should not pretend to beat every alternative at everything; it should focus on the decision-quality gap.

**[Role #32]**: Decision-Quality Moat
_Concept_: The app must be uniquely good at recommending the right routine and products based on ingredients, user needs, budget, local availability, and outcome history.
_Novelty_: The moat is product decision quality and user memory, not content virality.

**[Role #33]**: Skin Recovery Mode
_Concept_: When a user reports irritation, worsening skin, or disappointment, the app should switch into recovery mode: assess symptom severity, pause the newest or riskiest product, simplify the routine, and update future recommendations.
_Novelty_: Bad outcomes become a trust-building moment because the app protects the user instead of pushing more products.

**[Role #34]**: Severity-Based Reaction Handling
_Concept_: Mild reactions can trigger temporary routine simplification and patch-test guidance; moderate reactions can trigger barrier recovery and monitoring; severe symptoms such as swelling, pain, open skin, blisters, spreading rash, or eye/lip involvement should trigger professional-care guidance.
_Novelty_: The app's response changes by risk level, which makes the safety logic more credible.

**[Role #35]**: Non-Defensive Safety Communication
_Concept_: The app should communicate that skin can react differently even to well-matched products, then focus on protecting the skin, identifying possible triggers, and improving future recommendations.
_Novelty_: The tone preserves user trust by acknowledging uncertainty without blaming the user or hiding behind disclaimers.

**Morphological Analysis:**

- **Interactive Focus:** Structuring the product into dimensions and choosing the best MVP combinations.
- **Key Breakthroughs:** The MVP input model should collect enough context for credible personalization without making onboarding too heavy.

**Captured Ideas:**

**[Morph #1]**: MVP Input Combination
_Concept_: The MVP should use a short questionnaire, current routine entry, budget, location/local market, product preferences, and reaction/outcome check-ins.
_Novelty_: This combination supports credible personalization and future learning while avoiding the complexity of barcode scanning, automated photo analysis, or long diagnostic onboarding.

**[Morph #2]**: MVP Recommendation Output Package
_Concept_: The MVP should output a skin profile summary, morning/evening routine, 2-3 product options per step, ingredient explanations, current routine conflict warnings, budget alternatives, local availability links, relevant safety warnings, and a 7/14/30-day check-in schedule.
_Novelty_: The output combines advice, reasoning, shopping support, and follow-up rather than only listing products.

**[Morph #3]**: Hybrid MVP Recommendation Engine
_Concept_: The MVP should use rule-based matching, a structured product database, AI-generated explanations, and manual review for edge cases.
_Novelty_: This balances personalization, safety, credibility, and build feasibility before investing in full automation or photo-based analysis.

**[Morph #4]**: Single-Market 300 Product Catalog
_Concept_: The MVP should launch with a curated catalog of about 300 products for one market, each tagged by category, routine step, INCI list, key actives, function tags, skin type fit, concern fit, caution tags, claims, price, size, cost per ml, local availability, product links, last verified date, and formula-change flags.
_Novelty_: A focused catalog is large enough to generate useful recommendations but small enough to verify manually and maintain quality.

**[Morph #5]**: MVP Safety Package
_Concept_: The MVP should include symptom triage, no medical diagnosis wording, ingredient caution warnings, routine conflict detection, patch-test guidance, slow-start sensitive-skin mode, skin recovery mode, dermatologist escalation, and flagged-case manual review.
_Novelty_: Safety is embedded into the recommendation flow rather than added as a generic disclaimer.

**[Morph #6]**: First Routine Free Monetization
_Concept_: The first full routine should be free to build trust, including personalized profile, morning/evening routine, product recommendations, ingredient explanations, safety warnings, budget options, local links, and the first 7-day check-in.
_Novelty_: The app proves recommendation quality before asking users to pay.

**[Morph #7]**: Premium Adaptive Advisor
_Concept_: Premium value should focus on ongoing adaptation: 14/30-day tracking, current routine conflict detection, product dupe finder, new product checker, ingredient conflict alerts, seasonal adjustments, product replacement assistant, sensitive-skin mode, photo progress journal, and ingredient/product monitoring.
_Novelty_: Users pay for continued intelligence and personalization, not for the first generic answer.
